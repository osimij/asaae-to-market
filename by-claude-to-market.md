# Tajikistan's IT market: a comprehensive entry guide for ASAAE Digital

**Tajikistan is rapidly building Central Asia's most ambitious AI and digital infrastructure, creating unprecedented entry opportunities for cybersecurity and AI firms.** The country declared 2025–2030 the "Years of Digital Economy and Innovation Development," adopted the region's first AI strategy, opened IT Park Dushanbe with near-zero tax rates, and secured a unanimous UN General Assembly resolution to establish a Regional AI Center in Dushanbe. Yet critical gaps persist — Tajikistan has no national CERT, ranks #1 in the CIS for cyber threats (**60.69%** of users affected), and banks operate without mandatory cybersecurity regulations. For ASAAE Digital (Chahon IT LLC), this combination of explosive government ambition and severe cybersecurity underinvestment represents a compelling market entry moment.

---

## IT Park Dushanbe offers the region's most generous tax regime

**IT Park Dushanbe** officially opened on **April 7, 2025**, under Government Decree No. 440 (July 31, 2024). Located on Mirzo Tursunzoda Street opposite the Russian-Tajik Slavic University, it operates under the Agency for Innovation and Digital Technologies under the President. As of mid-2025, **18 resident companies** have joined, including Alif (fintech leader), zypl.ai (AI/ML), and Planet9.

The tax regime is exceptionally favorable — effectively a near-zero tax environment for IT companies:

| Tax | Standard rate | IT Park resident rate |
|-----|--------------|----------------------|
| Corporate profit tax | 15% | **0%** |
| VAT | 18% | **0%** |
| Dividend tax | 8–13% | **0%** |
| Local taxes | Various | **0%** |
| Personal income tax | 8–13% | **6%** |
| Social tax | 25% | **1%** |
| Import duties on innovative equipment | Standard rates | **0%** |

To qualify, companies must be registered as a legal entity or individual entrepreneur in Tajikistan, operate in one of **22 approved innovation areas** (which include AI, cybersecurity, cloud, and blockchain), submit a detailed business plan in Tajik, and demonstrate no outstanding tax debts. Applications are reviewed within 5 days by a special commission. A new IT Park building is planned with **Republic of Korea** cooperation, and in December 2025, IT Park Dushanbe signed a **trilateral cooperation agreement** with IT Park Uzbekistan and Astana Hub (Kazakhstan) covering AI, cybersecurity collaboration, and joint access to regional markets.

Tajikistan also maintains **five Free Economic Zones** (Sughd, Dangara, Pyanj, Ishkashim, Kulob) with generous tax holidays, though the IT Park regime is specifically designed for technology companies and offers superior benefits. A new **Investment Law** signed May 14, 2025 (No. 2173) further promotes innovation-focused investment with improved legal protections.

## The digital economy push is backed by real government action and international funding

President Emomali Rahmon signed **Presidential Decree No. 911** on January 8, 2025, formally launching the "Years of Digital Economy and Innovation Development" (2025–2030). The initiative rests on seven pillars: regulatory modernization, digital infrastructure (including 5G), full digitalization of public services, human capital development, AI deployment, cybersecurity, and digital entrepreneurship. The government claims over **70% of government services** have been converted to electronic format, though the **UN E-Government Development Index** ranks Tajikistan **123rd out of 193 countries** (score 0.56), suggesting significant implementation gaps remain.

International development financing is substantial. The **World Bank** approved a **$39 million** Digital Foundations Project in December 2024 (IDA $30M + Switzerland $9M), targeting digital skills for over **28,000 individuals** and enhanced digital public services. A separate **$50 million** IDA grant (August 2025) supports telecom sector competition and governance reform. The **ADB** maintains 18 active projects totaling **$930 million** and has explicitly called for "robust digital public infrastructure." Russia committed **2.5 billion rubles** (~$25M) to joint digitalization projects starting October 2024, while the **EU** is investing **€60 million** in satellite broadband for Central Asia.

The macro backdrop is strong. GDP grew **8.4%** in 2024, with projections of 7.0–7.4% for 2025. Total foreign investment reached **$4.9 billion** in 2024, a 73% increase. Standard & Poor's upgraded Tajikistan to **"B" (Stable)**, and Moody's shifted its outlook to positive. However, challenges persist: internet costs remain among the highest in Central Asia at **$1.65/GB** (versus $0.17 in Kyrgyzstan), mobile internet speed ranks **139th globally**, and the state-owned Tajiktelecom maintains a monopoly on international bandwidth.

## Tajikistan's AI ambitions are the most aggressive in Central Asia

The **Strategy for Development of Artificial Intelligence until 2040**, adopted via Government Resolution No. 483 on September 30, 2022, made Tajikistan the first country in Central Asia — and the first low-income country globally — to adopt a national AI strategy. The core target is for AI to contribute **5% of GDP by 2040**. Implementation is coordinated by the **AI Council** under the Ministry of Industry and New Technologies, chaired by Azizjon Azimi (CEO of zypl.ai, Knight-Hennessy Scholar).

**Area AI 1.0**, launched June 25, 2025, is positioned as the "world's first Special Artificial Intelligence Zone" — a technopark and cluster in Dushanbe's Darvoz district built by local company darya.ai. It encompasses five zones: AI education, a startup hub, fundamental/applied research, a green data center (hydropower-powered), and a regional regulatory coordination center. The **Area AI Investment Consortium**, formed at AI Conf 2025 (October 25, 2025), includes founding members zypl.ai, epsilon3.ai, zehnlab.ai, darya.ai, and Qulla AI, with international partners from the US, UAE, Singapore, India, and beyond. Partnerships have been signed with **Perplexity AI, Google DeepMind, NVIDIA, Yotta (India), Presight/G42 (UAE), Scale AI, and Mistral AI**. Total agreements at AI Conf 2025 reached **$117 million**.

The **Academy of AI** (aiacademy.tj), founded by TajRupt and linked to Azizjon Azimi, has trained **700+ AI specialists** as of October 2025, with a target of 2,000 under **Project Soro** — a UNICEF-partnered initiative targeting **4,000 schools and 2 million students** with a projected **$150 million** economic impact over five years. Key AI products include **SoroLLM** (27-billion-parameter language model for Tajik), **zGAN** (generative AI for credit scoring, deployed by 30+ banks in 10+ countries), and the **Comet AI Browser** (agent-based AI browser for government, piloted with 140 Ministry employees).

On July 25, 2025, the **UN General Assembly unanimously adopted** Tajikistan's resolution on "The Role of AI in Creating New Opportunities for Sustainable Development in Central Asia." The resolution proposes establishing a **Regional AI Center in Dushanbe** and outlines six mechanisms: joint AI education, coordinated research, shared data centers, harmonized AI governance, self-regulation frameworks, and collaboration with international organizations. This gives Tajikistan unique diplomatic positioning as Central Asia's AI policy leader.

## Cybersecurity gaps are severe — and this is where the opportunity lies

**Tajikistan leads the CIS in cyber vulnerability.** A 2024 Kaspersky Lab study found **60.69%** of Tajik users encountered cyber threats — the highest rate across all CIS nations. The finance sector absorbs **28%** of targeted attacks, followed by construction (24%) and manufacturing (14%). Across the CIS, attacks surged **2.6x** in Q2 2024 versus Q2 2023, with threat groups like YoroTrooper and SneakyChef specifically targeting the region. The most common attack vectors against financial organizations are phishing emails, ransomware, and DDoS attacks, with API exploitation and supply chain attacks expected to dominate in 2025–2026.

The institutional cybersecurity framework has critical deficiencies. **Tajikistan lacks a formal national CERT** — unlike Kazakhstan (KZ-CERT), Uzbekistan (UZ-CERT), and Kyrgyzstan, which all operate functioning incident response centers. A July 2025 eGA Cybersecurity Baseline Study covering Central Asia found Tajikistan has "significant capacity gaps" in incident response, institutional coordination, and public awareness. Banking legislation **contains no mandatory regulations** for electronic asset security or data storage; the National Bank of Tajikistan treats international security standards like COBIT as suggestions rather than requirements. A **National Cybersecurity Plan** was adopted in 2024, but implementation details remain unclear.

The cybersecurity vendor landscape is dominated by Russian firms — **Kaspersky Lab** (endpoint security), **Positive Technologies** (threat research and products), and **Dr.Web** (antivirus). **Noventiq** (formerly Softline) operates from Dushanbe offering cybersecurity, AI, and cloud services. Local system integrators include **IT Group Tajikistan** (itgroup.tj), **Pixel LLC** (pixel.tj, founded 2013), **iTservice** (won Best IT Company award 2024), and **SIB** (information security). No dedicated local cybersecurity firm of significant scale appears to operate in the market. The **Association of Financial Organizations of Tajikistan (AFOT)**, covering approximately **90% of the financial market** (15 banks, 16 MDOs, and others), explicitly identified **strengthening cybersecurity** as a strategic priority for 2025.

For banking-sector-specific demand: **Alif Bank** completed international penetration testing by Snapsec (UK) in 2024 and implemented Visa Risk Manager, while the **International Bank of Tajikistan** deployed AI-based credit scoring that reduced risk assessment costs by **34%** and improved default prediction accuracy to **88%**. These early adopters signal growing institutional appetite for professional cybersecurity services, but most banks have yet to invest in serious security infrastructure.

## Banking digitalization is creating urgent demand for security services

The banking sector is growing explosively. Total assets reached **54.9 billion somoni** in 2025, up **36.6%** year-over-year. The system comprises **65 credit institutions** including 16 commercial banks. Banking cards in circulation reached **9.1 million** (up 20.5% from H1 2024), and in H1 2025 alone, **56.3 million** non-cash transactions worth over **$2 billion** were processed. Cashless payments reached **38.9%** of total turnover by December 2025, up 9.4 percentage points year-over-year, with expert forecasts projecting **55–65%** by 2030.

**Alif Bank** dominates the fintech landscape with **2.2 million+ active clients**, **50%+ share of all Visa payments** in Tajikistan, $64 million run-rate revenue, and operations expanding to Uzbekistan, Pakistan, and the UAE. **Freedom Bank Tajikistan** (licensed October 2024) launched the country's first digital mortgage and grew its loan portfolio **173.3%** in Q4 2025 alone. Two major microfinance organizations — **Humo** and **Imon International** — received full bank licenses in 2025, immediately entering the top 10 by assets. The competitive landscape is shifting from traditional banking toward fintech-driven digital services, increasing the attack surface and cybersecurity requirements across the sector.

The **Korti Milli** national payment system and **HUMO** (Uzbekistan's system) signed a strategic integration partnership at the Digital Tajikistan 2025 forum (October 13, 2025), enabling cross-border card acceptance. Google Pay launched in Tajikistan in 2024. Remittances reached a record **$5.8 billion** in 2024 (45% of GDP), creating massive flows through digital channels. Each of these developments expands the digital payment ecosystem while amplifying fraud and cybersecurity risks.

## Digital Tajikistan 2025 signaled the market's maturation

The **2nd International PLUS-Forum "Digital Tajikistan 2025"** took place on **October 13, 2025** in Dushanbe, supported by the Agency for Innovation and Digital Technologies, the National Bank of Tajikistan, and the Communication Service. It drew **1,226 delegate applications from 30 countries** and featured **100+ speakers**. Huawei served as title sponsor, with DC City Dushanbe as general sponsor and IBS Project as diamond sponsor.

Key forum outcomes included the **HUMO–Korti Milli strategic partnership** for cross-border payment integration, the IDC "White Book" on Tajikistan's digital development, and substantive sessions on cybersecurity threats — where HUMO's cybersecurity chief warned that generative AI is being weaponized by criminals. Forum topics spanned AI in payments, blockchain regulation, green digital transformation, e-commerce logistics, open banking APIs, and workforce development. The Uzbekistan delegation was led by **Minister of Digital Technologies Sherzod Shermatov**, underscoring the event's regional significance. A dedicated session on **"Cybersecurity of State Infrastructure, Economy, Banking and Society"** highlights the growing institutional focus on this area.

## The IT workforce is small but rapidly expanding

Tajikistan's IT talent pool remains constrained but is growing through multiple channels. The country has **38 higher education institutions**, with dedicated IT programs at the Tajik Technical University, the Technological University of Tajikistan (which has a Faculty of Information-Communication and Artificial Intelligence), and others. However, a historical estimate suggested only **10% of IT graduates were employable** upon graduation — a quality gap the government is addressing.

Average IT salaries range from **~$200/month** for entry-level programmers to **$920–1,080/month** at leading firms like Alif Bank (where senior roles can reach $3,000+/month). The national average salary is approximately **$240–314/month**, placing experienced IT professionals at 3–4x the median. This is dramatically below regional competitors: Kazakhstan IT medians run $300–1,500+/month, and Russian IT salaries average ~$1,800/month, driving significant **brain drain** — over 1 million Tajik citizens work abroad, predominantly in Russia.

Training initiatives are scaling rapidly. The **AI Academy** has trained 700+ specialists. **Alif Academy** has graduated 2,000+ programmers. The World Bank's Digital Foundations Project targets **28,000 individuals** for digital skills training. **Project Soro** aims to train 2,000 AI specialists and introduce AI curriculum across 4,000 schools. NVIDIA's Deep Learning Institute is providing educational materials through the AI Council. IT-RUN Academy operates coding programs in Dushanbe and Khujand. These programs, combined with IT Park tax incentives, are designed to stanch outflow and build a local talent pipeline, though the gap between supply and demand remains substantial — especially in cybersecurity, where no dedicated local training programs appear to exist.

## Competitive landscape and strategic positioning for ASAAE Digital

The competitive analysis reveals a fragmented market with **no dominant local cybersecurity specialist**. Russian vendors (Kaspersky, Positive Technologies) provide products but limited on-the-ground integration services. Noventiq offers broader IT services including cybersecurity from its Dushanbe office. Local integrators (IT Group, Pixel, iTservice) focus on infrastructure and general IT rather than specialized cybersecurity or AI. **Huawei** dominates telecom infrastructure, having built 7,600 sites, but its cybersecurity offerings in Tajikistan appear limited to network-level security.

The government is actively seeking partners for both AI and cybersecurity implementation. The trilateral IT Park agreement (Tajikistan–Uzbekistan–Kazakhstan) explicitly mentions AI and cybersecurity collaboration. Russia's 2.5 billion ruble commitment and the CIS digital evidence exchange system indicate geopolitical competition for influence in Tajikistan's digital infrastructure — offering multiple potential alignment paths for an entering firm.

For ASAAE Digital, the clearest market entry opportunities include:

- **Banking sector cybersecurity**: AFOT's strategic priority on cybersecurity, combined with the absence of mandatory regulations (creating advisory/compliance demand) and rapid digital payments growth, positions this as the highest-value near-term segment
- **Government cybersecurity infrastructure**: The absence of a national CERT, the new National Cybersecurity Plan (2024), and massive World Bank/ADB-funded digitalization projects create demand for institutional security architecture
- **AI integration services**: The AI Strategy 2040, Area AI zone, and Project Soro are generating demand for applied AI implementation that outpaces local capacity
- **IT Park residency**: The 0% tax regime, combined with the 22 qualifying activity categories (including AI and cybersecurity), makes IT Park Dushanbe the logical operational base

## Conclusion

Tajikistan presents a rare convergence: a government pouring political capital and international funding into digital transformation while fundamentally lacking the cybersecurity infrastructure to protect it. The market is small — total telecom revenues run ~$400 million annually, and e-commerce remains nascent at under $30 million — but growth rates are exceptional (banking assets up 36.6%, cards up 20.5%, GDP up 8.4%). The AI ambitions, anchored by the UN General Assembly resolution and Area AI zone, are creating regional credibility that could attract disproportionate investment. For a firm specializing in AI and cybersecurity, the timing is strategic: infrastructure is being built now, regulatory frameworks are still being written, and the institutional relationships being formed today will shape the market for a decade. The key risk factors — internet infrastructure costs, bureaucratic complexity, brain drain, and heavy Russian/Chinese vendor presence — are real but navigable, particularly through IT Park residency and alignment with the government's stated digital economy priorities.
